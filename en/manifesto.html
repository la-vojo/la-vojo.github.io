Manifesto

The Way·Manifesto·Tradition·Method

There are questions we no longer dare ask aloud. Not because they are forbidden. Because they seem naïve, or too large, or because the available answers no longer suit us - neither those of the instituted religions, nor those of a materialism that claims the question itself is a mistake.

Why am I here?

What remains when you take away everything you own?

How do you love someone you do not understand?

What do I do with my death?

These questions are not new. They were asked in the alleys of Galilee, two thousand years ago, by a man who wrote nothing, who founded no institution, who named no successor. He simply said: seek.

What we have lost

The texts that report his words had a strange fate. Some were kept, others set aside - not because they were less true, but because they suited less well an organization that needed clear borders, stable hierarchies, a designated enemy, and a promise of reward after death.

This filtering had a cost.

It produced a jealous and punishing God of whom Yeshua himself hardly spoke. It erased women from the transmission - beginning with the one who, in every account, was the first to see and to bear witness. It turned a teaching about the presence of the divine in the ordinary into an institution that mediates access to the divine and sets its price.

What we propose is not a reform of what exists. It is a return to what precedes it.

What the texts really say

We work on a deliberately narrow corpus - the oldest gospels, including those the Church set aside in the second century: Thomas, Mary Magdalene, Judas.

We read them in translations made as close as possible to the original manuscripts - ancient Greek and Sahidic Coptic - directly into Esperanto, without passing through an intermediary national language. The local translation, where it exists, is made from Esperanto - not from French or English. No community holds an advantage over another.

This philological work is not an academic curiosity. It is a necessity: every translation is an interpretation, and every interpretation is a theology. We want to hear what was said before others decided what it was supposed to mean.

What we hear, when we listen without the accumulated filters, is at once simpler and more demanding than what the institutions made of it.

Simpler: the Logos - the organizing principle of all that exists - is not beyond the world. It is in the wood you split and the stone you lift. No intermediary is needed to find it.

More demanding: no one can walk this way for you. No declaration of allegiance, no ritual, no belonging saves you from yourself. What is within you and that you have not yet brought to birth will in the end be what you lack.

What we do

We meet each week. We read a short text - a single saying, sometimes two lines. We let it resonate in silence. Then we speak, or we do not speak. We eat together.

Four times a year, at the solstices and equinoxes, we celebrate the cycles of the Earth - because we live on this planet and its rhythm is ours, whether we want it or not.

We have no clergy. No compulsory dues. No dogma whose acceptance conditions belonging. No promise of paradise nor threat of hell.

We have texts, a practice, and one conviction: that life does not belong to us - that it passes through us - and that passing through it together is worth more than pretending the question does not arise.

What we are not

We are not a therapy. If you need psychological help, consult a professional.

We are not a network. If you are looking for professional contacts or social visibility, you will do better elsewhere.

We are not a substitute family. We do not claim to replace those close to you, nor to cut you off from them.

We are not the only ones who are right. Other traditions carry truths that we do not carry. We have no list of enemies.

What we are

People who think that certain questions deserve to be asked seriously, regularly, in company.

People who think that eating together and walking together are spiritual acts - not because we give them that name, but because they create the conditions in which something real can happen between people.

People who think that the light enlightens every human being coming into the world - without exception, without condition, without needing to pronounce any particular name to gain access to it.

The way is open to all who walk.
It belongs to no one.
Not even to us.

It is now for you to see whether you are seeking something in this direction.
