The Way·Manifesto·Tradition·Method

Why "tradition"

La Vojo is not an invention. It is a return. Its texts are two thousand years old. Its questions are the ones human beings have asked since they began asking questions at all. Its central practice - gathering, reading, eating together - is that of the first communities, who called themselves hoi tês hodou: those of the Way. Even before the word "Christian."

The historical lineage

The 1st century

Yeshua of Nazareth teaches in Galilee. He leaves no writing. His words are memorized, transmitted orally, then gradually set down in writing. The first communities call themselves hoi tês hodou - those of the Way (Acts 9:2).

The 1st–2nd century

Several corpora of texts circulate in the communities of the Mediterranean basin. Among them: the Gospel of Thomas, the Gospel of Mary Magdalene, the Gospel of Judas - and the texts that will become the canonical gospels.

The 2nd century - Marcion

Marcion of Sinope proposes, as early as 144, a purified reading of the texts: without the Old Testament, without the jealous and punishing God, centered on the teaching of Yeshua. He is declared a heretic and excommunicated. La Vojo grants him a sound intuition, without adopting the whole of his theology - in particular his docetism (a Yeshua with no real body) and his demiurgic dualism, both incompatible with John 1:3 and John 1:14.

2nd–4th century - the filtering

The councils select, set aside, destroy. The texts not retained are hidden or lost. Marcion's own writings disappear: they are known only through the refutations of Tertullian.

1945 - Nag Hammadi

An Egyptian farmer, by chance, uncovers a sealed jar containing thirteen codices in Sahidic Coptic, dating from the 4th century. Among them: the complete Gospel of Thomas, the Gospel of Philip, the Gospel of Truth. What the Church had set aside reappears.

1970-2006 - Mary and Judas

The Gospel of Mary is edited and translated. The Codex Tchacos, containing the Gospel of Judas, is published in 2006. The corpus of La Vojo is now complete and accessible.

Today - La Vojo

La Vojo does not claim to be the sole heir of this teaching. It claims only to read these texts with rigor and honesty, in a language that belongs to no one, and to draw from them a coherent practice for human beings living now.

An objection: Peter and the Church

The objection comes quickly, and it is legitimate. Matthew 16:18 has Yeshua say: "You are Peter, and on this rock I will build my Church." How can one maintain that he founded no institution and named no successor?

Here is what an examination of the texts allows us to establish.

The scene exists in triple, the saying in single. Peter's confession at Caesarea Philippi is told by Mark (8:27-30), Luke (9:18-21) and Matthew (16:13-20). Same place, same question, same answer from Peter. But the saying about the rock and the Church appears only in Matthew. Now Mark is the oldest, and Matthew uses it as a source: the passage was therefore added to a narrative that did not contain it.

The word ekklêsia appears twice in four gospels. Matthew 16:18 and 18:17. Never in Mark, nor in Luke, nor in John. A founder of a Church would likely have spoken of it more often, and not in a single text. The term itself is Greek civic vocabulary - the assembly of citizens - and has no religious meaning in Aramaic.

Five verses later, Peter becomes the obstacle. Matthew 16:23: "Get behind me, Satan, you are a stumbling block to me." The Greek word is skandalon - the stone of stumbling. To pass from foundation to obstacle in seven verses is a singular treatment for a designated successor.

The wordplay exists only in Greek. Petros, masculine, means a pebble; petra, feminine, the bedrock. The Aramaic that Yeshua spoke has only one word, Kepha. Either the Greek distinction is deliberate, and "this rock" designates Peter's confession rather than his person; or the wordplay is a later Greek construction. In both cases, it is not in the mouth of a man speaking Aramaic.

The set-aside texts point to someone else. Thomas logion 12 poses exactly the question of succession: the disciples ask who will lead them after Yeshua's departure. The answer points to James the Just, not Peter. Logion 13 puts Peter in a position of failure: his answer is surpassed by that of Thomas, whom Yeshua takes aside. The Gospel of Mary shows Peter refusing that a woman received a higher revelation - and Levi answering him that if the Savior judged her worthy, no one has standing to reject her.

What La Vojo concludes from this, and what it does not. The question is not settled. Catholic exegesis holds Matthew 16:18 to be authentic and sees in it the scriptural foundation of papal primacy. Majority historical criticism sees in it a formulation of the Matthean community of the years 80-90, when questions of authority were becoming pressing. Both readings have their arguments, and La Vojo has no authority to settle a debate that two thousand years of scholarship have not closed.

What it simply observes: the evidence converges. The silence of Mark and Luke, the rarity of the word ekklêsia, the immediate reversal into skandalon, the Aramaic impossibility of the wordplay, and three independent texts that know no founder Peter.

A remark of proportion, finally. Even holding the verse to be authentic, to designate a man to lead a community is not to found an institution endowed with a clergy, a magisterium and a doctrine of exclusive salvation. Between the saying at Caesarea and the papacy lie fifteen centuries of construction that this verse alone does not carry.

La Vojo does not build on this question. It poses it, gives what it knows, and leaves each person to conclude.

A lesson from Marcion

The total destruction of the Marcionite writings is a warning. A tradition without its own preserved written corpus is vulnerable. The project of translation into Esperanto is not only philological - it is a protection of the tradition against its own possible disappearance.
