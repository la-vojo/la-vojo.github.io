Why translate anew

Every translation is a theology. Jerome's Vulgate (382-405) produced Roman Catholicism. The King James Bible (1611) produced English Protestantism. The Esperanto Sankta Biblio (1926) was translated from secondary sources, with Protestant assumptions.

La Vojo translates de novo from the source languages — Koine Greek and Sahidic Coptic — so as not to inherit the theological choices of earlier translators. We want to hear what was said before others decided what it was supposed to mean.

What common translations have lost

Five examples, among many others. Each time: the original term, the common translation, and what the source language actually says.

Logos (λόγος)

"the Word" → the organizing principle of all that exists

"Word" is a calque of the Latin verbum: it points toward a God who speaks and legislates. But among the Greek philosophers — Heraclitus, the Stoics — the logos is the reason that governs the cosmos, the principle that orders reality. It is that far broader sense that the opening of John carries.

Ptôchoi tô pneumati (πτωχοὶ τῷ πνεύματι)

"the poor in spirit" → the beggars of breath

Ptôchos is not the modest poor man — it is the beggar, the one who holds out a hand, the one who has nothing. Not a gentle virtue of intellectual humility, but a condition of radical lack. The beggars of breath are those who know they are not complete, and who seek because they lack.

Splanchnizomenos (σπλαγχνιζόμενος)

"moved with compassion" → shaken in the guts

The word comes from splanchna — the entrails, the viscera. The compassion Luke describes is not a polite, distant emotion: it is a physical, painful reaction, felt in the gut. "Moved with compassion" is too mild for what the Greek says.

Monogenês (μονογενής)

"the only son" → one of a kind

Monos (only) + genos (kind, genus). "Only son" presupposes a theology of exclusive sonship that is not in the etymology. The word says: unique in its kind, without equivalent.

Entos humôn (ἐντὸς ὑμῶν)

"within you" → within you / among you (unresolved ambiguity)

Entos means "inside of" (an inner sense) as much as "in the midst of, among" (a communal sense). Both readings are valid and complementary: the Kingdom is inner and it manifests between people. Where translations decide, La Vojo deliberately keeps the ambiguity — because it is in the text.

Why Esperanto

A language of no one, and therefore a language of all. Esperanto was created by Ludwik Zamenhof in 1887 as a project of peace: a constructed, neutral language that no one owns as a mother tongue and that anyone can learn on equal footing.

Translating directly from the original manuscripts into Esperanto, without passing through an intermediary national language, prevents one culture from imposing its choices on the others. The local translation, where it exists, is made from Esperanto — not from French or English. No community holds an advantage over another. The canonical language belongs to no one: that is precisely why it can be shared.
